Chapter 3 Univariate Linear Regression

Univariate linear regression is a statistical technique used to model and analyze the relationship between a single independent variable and a dependent variable. It is based on the linear equation:

\[ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1x + \epsilon \]

where: - \(y\) is the dependent variable. - \(x\) is the independent variable. - \(\beta_0\) is the y-intercept. - \(\beta_1\) is the slope of the line. - \(\epsilon\) represents the error term.

3.1 Key Concepts

3.1.1 Slope (\(\beta_1\))

The slope indicates the change in the dependent variable for each unit change in the independent variable.

3.1.2 Y-intercept (\(\beta_0\))

The y-intercept is the value of \(y\) when \(x\) is zero, representing the starting point of the line in the regression model.

3.1.3 Error Term (\(\epsilon\))

The error term accounts for the variability in \(y\) that cannot be explained by the linear relationship with \(x\).

3.2 Model Estimation

The coefficients \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\) are estimated using least squares method, which minimizes the sum of squared differences between observed and predicted values.

3.3 Interpretation

Interpreting the results involves examining the estimated coefficients and assessing the model’s fit and predictive power.

3.4 Assumptions

The application of linear regression relies on several key assumptions:

  1. Linearity: The relationship between the independent and dependent variable is linear.

  2. Independence: Observations are independent of each other.

  3. Homoscedasticity: The residuals (differences between observed and predicted values) have constant variance across all levels of the independent variable.

  4. Normality: The residuals are normally distributed.

Understanding these aspects of univariate linear regression is crucial for correctly applying the method and interpreting its results.

3.5 Goodness-of-Fit: R-squared (R²)

R-squared is a statistical measure that quantifies the proportion of the variance in the dependent variable that is predictable from the independent variable. It ranges from 0 to 1, where higher values indicate a better fit of the model.

\[ R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \bar{y})^2} \]

where \(\hat{y}_i\) is the predicted value and \(\bar{y}\) is the mean of \(y\).

Univariate linear regression provides a simple yet powerful way to understand and predict relationships between two variables.
